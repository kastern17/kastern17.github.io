Giuseppe Verdi (1813 - 1901)

Giuseppe Verdi was more than a composer; he was a force of nature in 19th-century Italy. Born in the small village of Le Roncole, Verdi rose from modest beginnings to become a central figure in Italian opera, with masterpieces like Rigoletto, La Traviata, and Aida cementing his legacy. His music resonated deeply with the Italian people, intertwining with the nation's cultural and political fabric during the Risorgimento, the movement for Italian unification. The chorus "Va, pensiero" from his opera Nabucco became an unofficial anthem for the cause, symbolizing the longing for freedom and unity.

Verdi's personal life was marked by profound tragedies, including the loss of his first wife and their two young children. These experiences of grief and resilience profoundly influenced his compositions, infusing them with emotional depth and authenticity. His Messa da Requiem stands as a testament to his ability to channel personal sorrow into universal expressions of mourning and hope.

Beyond the stage, Verdi was a man of the people. He served briefly in the Italian parliament and was known for his philanthropic efforts, including the establishment of a retirement home for musicians in Milan. His commitment to both his art and his country made him a beloved national figure.

As we experience Verdi's Requiem, we are reminded of the man behind the music - Verdi was a composer whose life and work continue to inspire and move audiences around the world.

Verdi’s Messa da Requiem: Operatic Grandeur in Sacred Form

Giuseppe Verdi’s Messa da Requiem is a towering fusion of sacred ritual and operatic fire - music of immense power, spiritual depth, and dramatic tension. Premiered on May 22, 1874, in the Church of San Marco in Milan under Verdi’s own baton, the work was composed in memory of Alessandro Manzoni, the revered Italian author of I Promessi Sposi. Verdi’s admiration for Manzoni was profound, and the Requiem stands not only as a tribute to a national hero but also as a personal outpouring of grief transformed into musical majesty.

The Requiem has its roots in an earlier endeavor. In 1868, following the death of Gioachino Rossini, Verdi proposed a collaborative Requiem involving several Italian composers. He contributed the final movement, Libera me, but the project fell apart before it could be performed. When Manzoni died five years later, Verdi revisited and expanded that music, ultimately weaving it into the heart of a full-length Requiem Mass to be performed on the first anniversary of Manzoni’s death. The result is a work that straddles worlds - profoundly liturgical in text, unmistakably theatrical in style.

Though cast in the traditional structure of the Roman Catholic funeral mass, Verdi’s Requiem bursts with operatic color and dramatic pacing. The orchestra is rich and expansive, featuring double winds, four bassoons, a piccolo, off-stage and on-stage trumpets, four horns, three trombones, and a cimbasso, along with timpani and a thundering bass drum. The orchestration is not mere embellishment - it plays an integral role in the storytelling. In the infamous Dies irae, massive fortissimo timpani and bass drum strokes evoke the terror of the Last Judgment, while off-stage trumpets in the Tuba mirum surround the audience in a sonic halo of apocalyptic alarm.

Verdi’s dynamic range in the score is extreme. Markings stretch from hushed pppp to explosive ffff, each dynamic shift infused with dramatic intent. These aren’t ornamental contrasts - they serve the emotional and rhetorical flow of the work. The opening Requiem aeternam emerges from near silence, setting a tone of stillness and reverence. But that serenity shatters moments later with the first shattering Dies irae, a motif that recurs throughout the Mass like a fearful memory returning unbidden.

Equally striking is Verdi’s writing for voices. The four soloists - soprano, mezzo-soprano, tenor, and bass - move fluidly between operatic expressiveness and chamber-like intimacy. In the Agnus Dei, the soprano and mezzo begin with a stark, unaccompanied duet in octaves, which the chorus and flutes gently mirror, lending the moment a sense of raw vulnerability. Later, in the Lux aeterna, three soloists sing a delicate a cappella trio that suggests timelessness through transparency. The Sanctus, by contrast, is an exuberant double fugue performed by a divided chorus, bursting with contrapuntal energy that rivals any operatic finale.

Despite its sacred text, the Requiem has largely found its home on the concert stage rather than in liturgical services. Its sheer scale, theatrical pacing, and expressive intensity demand a setting where the music can resonate fully - both physically and emotionally. Hans von Bülow famously dismissed it as “an opera in clerical dress”, a jab that nonetheless highlights the boldness of Verdi’s approach. Yet it is precisely this fusion of forms - sacred gravity and operatic immediacy - that gives the work its enduring force.

Recent performances underscore its undiminished impact. A 2024 BBC Proms performance under Ryan Bancroft was praised for its “blazing force and compelling drama”, while Riccardo Muti’s 2025 appearance at London’s Royal Festival Hall was hailed as a “major musical event”, with demand for tickets surpassing supply. These reactions are not new. From its first performance, the Requiem has captivated audiences - stirring awe, reflection, and, often, tears.

Verdi’s Messa da Requiem stands today as a masterwork not just of 19th-century choral music, but of the entire Western canon. Its success lies in its refusal to compromise: it honors its sacred text while allowing the full power of musical drama to illuminate its themes. At its heart, it is a deeply human work - one that grapples with grief, fear, hope, and transcendence. In setting Manzoni’s memory to music, Verdi created something larger than a monument: he gave us a mirror through which we see our own mortality, and a voice—our own voice—crying and pleading into God’s eternity.

Farrell, Diana, soprano

Praised for her “golden voice” and dramatic range, American lyric-spinto soprano Diana Farrell is the 2023 American Prize laureate (Women in Opera) and winner of the 2024 American Prize for Professional Opera as well as the AIMS Vocal Competition. She made her Carnegie Hall debut in January 2025, creating Jane in the world premiere of The Yellow Wallpaper, fresh on the heels of her fourth Puccini’s Tosca (2024), and acclaimed portrayals of Lady Macbeth (Macbeth) and Krystyna Zywulska in Jake Heggie’s Two Remain. Her versatility spans Magda Sorel (The Consul), Contessa (Le nozze di Figaro), Micaëla (Carmen), and Anna Maurrant (Street Scene) with companies such as Opera Western Reserve, Crested Butte Music Festival, and Pacific Opera Project. On the concert stage she has headlined Beethoven’s Ninth, Berlioz’s Les Nuits d’été, Strauss’s Vier Lieder, and Mozart’s Requiem. Also an award-winning stage director and librettist, Diana serves as Artistic Director of Lyric Opera of Orange County and holds an Artist Diploma from the Cleveland Institute of Music.

Wesley, Alexis, mezzo-soprano

Cuban-American mezzo-soprano Alexis Wesley is a dynamic and versatile performer whose work spans opera, oratorio, musical theatre, and film. She has sung leading roles with Pacific Opera Project, Mission Opera, and Independent Opera Company, appearing in critically acclaimed productions of Dialogues des Carmélites, The Medium, Madama Butterfly, and Le nozze di Figaro. Her oratorio credits include solo appearances in Verdi’s Requiem, Handel’s Messiah, Mendelssohn’s Elijah, and Pergolesi’s Stabat Mater with ensembles across Los Angeles County. A frequent collaborator with new music and interdisciplinary projects, she has also produced and starred in original works for stage and screen. Alexis is a graduate of the Stella Adler Conservatory of Acting in New York and holds a Master of Music in Voice from California State University, Northridge.

Oh, Wi Young, tenor

Tenor Wi Young Oh is an internationally active soloist and conductor, widely recognized for his commanding voice and stylistic versatility. He has performed leading roles at some of the world’s most prominent venues, including Walt Disney Concert Hall, the Seoul Arts Center, and Lotte Concert Hall. His operatic credits include Samson (Samson and Delilah), Moses (Exodus), and Yoon Dong Joo in the eponymous opera at Disney Hall. As a tenor soloist, he has appeared in Beethoven’s Symphony No. 9 with the LAKMA Orchestra, and in numerous international peace concerts under the baton of composer Lim Goong Soo. In addition to his solo work, he serves as conductor of the Hankuk University of Foreign Studies Choir, KAMA Choir, and Golden Chorale, and teaches voice at W.M.U. His academic training includes advanced degrees in theology and music from institutions in Korea and the United States.

Lowe, Ben, baritone

Baritone Ben Lowe is a fast-rising artist known for his resonant, powerful voice and compelling stage presence. His recent European debut as the bass soloist in Verdi’s Requiem in Siena and Florence garnered critical acclaim, adding to a growing list of major international engagements. In the U.S., he has performed at New York’s Merkin Hall, the Broad Stage in Santa Monica, and with companies including LA Opera, OperaDelaware, and Opera UCLA. Notable appearances include roles in Dead Man Walking, Trial by Jury, and Matt Aucoin’s Crossing. A laureate of the Marilyn Horne Song Competition and a finalist in the Palm Springs Opera Guild and Loren L. Zachary Society Competitions, Ben studied under legendary baritone Vladimir Chernov. He holds degrees from UCLA and Cal State Dominguez Hills and is represented by Pinnacle Arts Management. His artistic range extends from grand opera to modern repertoire and concert works.

Dr. Maxim Kuzin, Music Director and Conductor

Dr. Maxim Kuzin, born in Ukraine, has garnered international acclaim in the orchestral and choral spheres. He is celebrated for his commitment to nurturing new talent, educational outreach, and uncovering hidden gems of classical music. His career spans many roles, including currently the Music Director of the Thousand Oaks Philharmonic, the UC Santa Barbara Chamber Orchestra, and the newly appointed Music Director of the Palisades Symphony. His influence extends to the Irvine Classical Players Youth Orchestra and the Hankuk University of Foreign Studies Chorale in Los Angeles. Dr. Kuzin has led National Symphony Orchestra of Ukraine, National Philharmonic Orchestra of Ukraine, National Symphony Orchestra of Georgia (Tbilisi), National Radio Symphony Orchestra of Ukraine, National President Orchestra of Ukraine, and Vladivostok Philharmonic Orchestra (Russia), among others. He received his doctoral degree in Orchestral Conducting at UCLA, where his mentor was Professor Neal Stulberg. Before moving to the United States in 2014, he was part of the vibrant music scene in Ukraine, contributing to the Tchaikovsky National Music Academy and the Reinhold Glière Institute of Music.

Susan S. Rosenstein, Choral Director

Susan S. Rosenstein, a gifted conductor, accompanist, pianist, organist, and teacher, has contributed to Los Angeles audiences and musicians in a variety of venues for over 50 years. As director of the Brentwood Palisades Chorale since 1987, Susan’s upbeat, engaging rehearsals have continued to draw members, both old and new, to the group. She has taught more than forty major choral works, including everybody’s favorite requiems (Mozart, Verdi, Brahms, Fauré), oratorios (Handel, Haydn, Bach, Mendelssohn), magnificats (Bach, Rutter), innumerable masses, Karl Orff’s Carmina Burana, Morten Lauridsen’s Lux Aeterna, and Ernest Bloch’s Avodath Hakodesh (Sacred Service). In addition to her work with the Chorale, Susan serves as Music Director of Wilshire Boulevard Temple and Associate Conductor of the SoCal Chorale, collaborating with Mary Lou Basaraba in preparing choirs for performances with the California Philharmonic, Golden State Pops Orchestra, Los Angeles Film Orchestra, and Culver City/Marina del Rey Symphony.

The Palisades Symphony was started in 1966 by Joel B. Lish. In 1965 Joel was hired to teach music at Palisades High School. Finding that he had some very good music students at Pali High, Joel started the orchestra in order to provide them with an additional opportunity to play orchestral music. In this endeavor, Joel was incredibly fortunate to meet Eva Holberg, a Pacific Palisades music-lover and volunteer, who became the President of the orchestra and ran it successfully for more than 50 years until her death at 91 in 2022.

After he retired from full-time teaching in 1995, Joel had more time to devote to his other career as a professional violist. He performed casual, strolling, chamber, and orchestral music with many ensembles, and he also played on many films and television shows. He conducted other groups, such as the Los Angeles Mandolin Orchestra and the Casa Italiana Opera Company. Joel also made wonderful arrangements for string ensembles—duets, trios and quartets.

The Palisades Symphony always stood at the center of Joel’s activities. It is a true community orchestra, as all musicians volunteer their services. Joel was gratified to know that his beloved orchestra would be able to continue under the very capable leadership of Denisa Hanna and Dr. Maxim Kuzin.

The Brentwood Palisades Chorale was founded in 1963 as one of the Adult Choruses of the City of Los Angeles. As such, the choir practiced under the direction of the late Frances Cain and performed not only with the Palisades Symphony, but also with the Brentwood Symphony, the Beach Cities Symphony, and the City of Los Angeles Combined Choruses at the Dorothy Chandler Pavilion. After the spring concert in 1987, Ms. Cain retired, and Susan S. Rosenstein was selected to be the Chorale’s director. The singers rehearse from September through May and perform with the orchestra each year in December and May. In its almost 60 years, the Chorale has performed with the Palisades Symphony and has introduced most of the classical choral literature to the Palisades and surrounding communities, all free of charge. The chorale will begin rehearsing its next concert at the end of this summer. New members are always welcome (no audition is required!) and may request information at any time by emailing Susan Rosenstein at brentwoodpalisadeschorale@gmail.com.
